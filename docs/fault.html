Fault

Fault is a zero-audio-input, eight-output CLAP instrument that turns a generated byte field, an arbitrary file, or the waveform ancestry of a PCM WAV into continuously changing audio. It combines raw-data scanning, stochastic breakpoint geometry, damaged codec models, and a bounded pressure, wavefold, and resonant-feedback stage. It can run continuously or respond as a velocity-sensitive MIDI instrument.

Workflow

  1. Set a REAPER track to eight channels and insert Fault.
  2. Choose a preset or begin with INIT. Use OPEN ANY to replace the generated field with any file. A recognized WAV opened from INIT selects WAVE TRACE.
  3. Use FREE for continuous sound, or select MIDI and play notes from the DAW.
  4. Set SCAN, GEOMETRY, CHAOS, and FOLD for the underlying motion.
  5. Choose a codec, then use RATE, BITS, and DAMAGE to break its continuity.
  6. Raise DRIVE, SHRED, and RESONANCE to expose the byte-field contours.
  7. Choose a ROUTE and set SPREAD for the eight-channel distribution.

A useful first experiment is a low displayed codec update rate with high FOLD, followed by enough DRIVE and SHRED to turn the held vectors into pronounced curves.

Performance

FREE preserves Fault's continuous generator behavior and bypasses the amplitude envelope. MIDI makes the same field engine a monophonic, last-note-priority voice. Note-on retriggers the attack from the current envelope level, velocity controls voice level, and releasing the active note returns to the most recently held note. When no notes remain, the release runs to silence and the field freezes once the envelope becomes idle.

MIDI note 60 (C4) is the patch's reference pitch: it retains the current SCAN speed and stochastic period behavior. Notes above or below it change traversal and breakpoint timing by equal-tempered semitone ratios, so an octave doubles or halves both clocks. This is relative pitch control rather than a promise of conventional oscillator tuning; byte structure, codecs, geometry, and loaded material can all pull the audible result away from a stable fundamental.

ControlMeaning
AAttack time from 1 ms to 5 seconds.
DDecay time from 5 ms to 8 seconds.
SSustain level from silence to full level.
RRelease time from 5 ms to 12 seconds.

The envelope graph is directly editable. Drag the attack and release endpoints horizontally, the decay breakpoint in both axes, and the sustain breakpoint vertically. The dashed live-level trace and highlighted breakpoint follow the active MIDI envelope stage.

PLAY and STOP remain global: they fade and freeze either mode. Presets, RANDOM PATCH, and MUTATE shape the timbre but leave the selected performance mode and ADSR settings alone.

Byte Field and Geometry

The default source is a deterministic 65,536-byte virtual field. GEN FIELD captures the currently selected CODEC as a hidden generation profile, rolls a new seed, and builds data chosen to expose that codec's characteristic behavior. The source readout shows the captured profile in brackets. Changing CODEC afterward does not rewrite the field, so the same material can be compared across codecs; press GEN FIELD again to tailor a new field to the new selection.

The profiles use different data grammars rather than post-processing the same noise: differential and predictive modes receive continuous slopes with controlled innovations; companders receive quiet detail and sparse peaks; speech-like modes receive voiced, residual, and excitation structure; DISC receives repeated blocks and markers; SUBBAND receives separated slow and alternating detail; TRANSFORM receives sparse short-block spectra; MODEM receives symbol plateaus; FAX receives repeated scanlines; and SIGMA 1-BIT receives slowly changing oversampled curves. For an arbitrary file, OPEN ANY reads from byte zero and maps every byte to an unsigned 8-bit bipolar sample.

With Decode WAVE data into eight lanes enabled in the open dialog, Fault parses RIFF chunks, jumps to the actual data chunk, and decodes integer PCM at 8, 16, 24, or 32 bits or floating-point PCM at 32 or 64 bits. It applies bounded gain recovery, remaps every source frame into eight related lanes, and requantizes those lanes into the byte field. Mono becomes eight short-offset traces; stereo becomes four related pairs; sources with eight or more channels retain eight native lanes. This preserves contour, rhythm, and some pitch identity without retaining a recoverable clean signal. Disable the option to include the WAV header and all other bytes in the older literal-byte treatment.

Raw files and the prepared eight-lane field are limited to 64 MiB. Longer sources show PARTIAL in the source readout. Internally, a shorter or irregularly sized source repeats far enough to form a power-of-two scan ring. The codec BITS control requantizes the field again later in the chain.

ControlMeaning
SCANSets traversal speed. Raw and generated fields report bytes per second. A prepared WAV reports a speed multiplier; 0.50 is the source's native frame rate and the full range extends from 1/256x to 256x.
TEXTUREChanges local smoothing and the blend among nearby bytes, structured regions, and metadata-like impulses.
GEOMETRYMorphs direct byte reading toward stochastic breakpoint segments and longer sample-to-sample connection curves.
CHAOSIncreases amplitude and period variation, octave-scale duration changes, irregular interpolation, and occasional rests.
FOLDApplies sine folding before the codec and nonlinear stages.
EVOLVERegenerates local regions near active read positions and crossfades them into the field. OFF preserves the current field; higher values shorten the nominal mutation interval.

EVOLVE is free-running rather than transport-synchronized. It changes regional content without replacing the whole field, so a patch can retain its identity while its local byte relationships drift.

Codec Damage

The codec section treats the generated or file-derived signal as material for destructive encoding. These modes are synthesis models, not interoperable media codecs:

  • PCM applies direct bit quantization.
  • DELTA quantizes the difference from a running predictor.
  • ADPCM adds an adaptive error step to the predictor path.
  • MU-LAW and A-LAW pass the signal through quantized companding curves.
  • CELP uses damaged pitch, gain, codebook, and packet-concealment behavior. It is not a G.729 encoder, decoder, or bitstream implementation.
  • DISC introduces block errors followed by interpolation, stale-data repetition, muting, or concealment fades inspired by optical-media playback. It does not implement CD or DVD framing, modulation, or error correction.
  • CVSD tracks the signal with a one-bit adaptive slope whose bit decisions, run lengths, and reconstruction smoothing can fail.
  • SUBBAND splits low and high content into independently damaged adaptive-prediction paths.
  • LPC replaces increasing amounts of prediction residual with pulse and noise excitation through an adaptive all-pole model.
  • TRANSFORM performs short block transforms, then quantizes, discards, or corrupts spectral coefficients before reconstruction.
  • PREDICT uses a second-order predictor with quantized residuals, missing corrections, and occasional residual upsets.
  • MODEM maps amplitude into damaged FSK-like symbols and carrier tones.
  • FAX maps level and slope into quadrature symbols, then exposes equalizer lag, phase slips, and training-like interruptions.
  • SIGMA 1-BIT uses an error-feedback one-bit stream followed by a deliberately under-resolved reconstruction filter.
  • HYBRID combines a predictive base layer with a correction layer that can thin, vanish, or invert.
ControlMeaning
RATESets the effective codec update rate. Most modes use it as a pre-encoding sample-and-hold; CVSD, MODEM, FAX, and SIGMA 1-BIT use it as a bit or symbol clock. Moving the control right lowers the displayed rate, reaching below 3 Hz at 48 kHz.
BITSSets an integer depth from 2 to 16 bits. In one-bit and carrier models it instead biases state count, tracking resolution, or reconstruction detail.
DAMAGERaises mode-specific prediction error, packet loss, stale blocks, coefficient removal, bit errors, phase slips, or correction-layer failure.

Integrated Nonlinear Stage

The shaping stage follows the codec so held steps and prediction errors become wave geometry rather than remaining quiet digital artifacts.

  • DRIVE raises level into pressure and saturation.
  • SHRED moves the nonlinear path toward stronger folding and edge generation.
  • RESONANCE raises a bounded internal feedback loop. Its tuning follows a smoothed spectral-center estimate of the generated material, while TEXTURE and CHAOS bias its color and response.
  • OUTPUT sets the final trim after internal calibration.

Feed-forward calibration and a slow, bounded level correction reduce large loudness jumps among codec and shaping settings. They do not normalize away transient or spectral differences. The final path removes DC and limits output to finite sample bounds.

Eight-Channel Routing

ROUTE changes how each output lane addresses the byte field:

  • PARALLEL keeps lanes near a shared read path.
  • DEINTERLEAVE distributes adjacent field data across eight lane-strided reads. For a prepared WAV, it follows the eight derived waveform lanes directly.
  • PLANES offsets lanes into separate field regions.
  • SHUFFLED uses repeatable hashed read addresses.
  • DIVERGENT gives lanes different strides and long-term trajectories.

SPREAD operates on the mean plus each channel's residual. At zero, all lanes collapse toward the instantaneous eight-channel mean. At one, their full differences remain. Bounded energy compensation prevents spread changes from acting mainly as gain changes.

The display shows eight recent output waveforms. Each trace scales itself to reveal shape and is therefore not an absolute level meter; use the peak readout or a following Multichannel Meter for level comparison.

Presets and Patch Actions

The preset menu contains thirteen complete starting points: INIT, SLOW FOLD, CODEC SCAR, DIVERGE, BREAKS, GLASS GRID, MU DUST, DELTA STAIRS, ADPCM TEAR, CELP CHOIR, LOW EMBERS, WIDE STATIC, and WAVE TRACE. Editing a sound control changes the menu state to CUSTOM.

  • PLAY resumes the field from its frozen position with a 20 ms fade-in.
  • STOP fades to silence over 20 ms, then freezes the synthesis engine without resetting it.
  • OPEN ANY loads any file as a byte field. Its enabled WAV option skips the container header and prepares eight lossy waveform-derived lanes; disabling the option uses every file byte literally.
  • GEN FIELD leaves file mode, captures the selected codec as the field profile, and creates new codec-oriented source data while retaining the remaining control settings.
  • RANDOM PATCH creates a new bounded patch across the field, codec, shaping, and routing sections.
  • MUTATE makes smaller changes around the current patch and usually preserves its seed and codec family.
  • UNDO restores the state before the most recent preset, source, field, randomize, or mutate action.

Preset, source, field, codec, bit-depth, and routing changes use a dual-engine crossfade instead of a hard reset. The automatable Run parameter mirrors the PLAY and STOP buttons. REAPER project state stores the transport state, performance mode, ADSR settings, complete patch, field seed, captured generation profile, and external source path; held notes and source bytes are not embedded. If the file is unavailable on recall, Fault falls back to the generated field and shows the missing source in its header. Sessions saved by preceding versions open in FREE mode and use their recalled codec as the initial field profile.
